Summary

The post discusses the complexities of serving large language models (LLMs) at scale, highlighting challenges such as non-deterministic outputs, sequential token generation, and distinct resource requirements during inference. It emphasizes that LLM inference is constrained more by memory than compute, with shifting resource profiles within a single request, and points to ongoing issues like non-determinism and GPU memory management. The post references frameworks and articles addressing these challenges, such as vLLM, SGLang, and work by Thinking Machines.

The post explains why high CPU usage alongside lower GPU utilization is common during vLLM-based LLM inference. It highlights that while the GPU handles compute-heavy tasks during the "Prefill" phase, the "Decode" phase is often memory-bound, and the CPU is heavily involved in orchestration tasks such as scheduling, tokenization, and sampling, as well as handling Python and async overhead from frameworks like FastAPI. Understanding these behaviors is crucial for optimizing infrastructure and throughput in LLM deployments.

The post critiques the hype around vLLM, especially among influencers who may not understand inference engines deeply, and provides a practical decision matrix for choosing between vLLM and SGLang based on workload characteristics. It highlights that SGLang outperforms vLLM for agentic, multi-turn, and prefix-reuse-heavy workloads due to its RadixAttention caching, while vLLM excels at high-concurrency, single-turn scenarios with its PagedAttention and batching. The key takeaway is to select your inference engine based on the specific needs of your workload, not popularity.
